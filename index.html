Hi!

I am a professional nerd, an avid keyboard user, amateur husband, and soon-to-be unemployed professional brain user.

Currently, I'm a post-proposal PhD candidate of Computer Science at Indiana University, specializing in Programming Languages (PL), minoring in Logic at the Department of Philosophy. I'm planning on finishing in late 2020. My advisor is Sam Tobin-Hochstadt.

I recently moved to Sandy, UT. I'm currently considering job opportunities, so you're more than welcome to send me an email is you're interested in working with me.

I like all abstractions at all levels; languages, functions, objects, sometimes types as well. Depending on the language I often prefer tail recursion to a loop, continuations to exceptions, and I equally like the stack and the heap (though I'm not very fond of the occasional GC pressure -- who is, right?). Every now and then I try to use my superpowers for the good of others.

I often get asked what does it mean to study/specialize on Programming Languages. The short answer is, you implement programs to solve problems, I implement languages to do that. The slightly longer answer is, studying programming langauges provides a unique perspectives on things like i) how programmers like to express computation to either machines or other programmers, ii) how programs get compiled and evaluated by real or virtual machines, iii) among the many possible ways of writing the same software, which one has the best performance in terms of space and time.

I prefer collaboration and team work to competition, (good) tea to (good) coffee, kindness to power. I equally and unconditionally like all the bi-pedals and poly-pods alike.

My undergraduate work was about adding tail-calls to a Racket to JS compiler. In my masters I studied Machine Learning and Natural Language Processing; my work was about developing a question answering system for high-school students. Currently (2019) my PhD work is about fast run-times for dynamic languages. In particular, I'm trying to show that rapid prototyping an efficient run-time for a self-hosting dynamically typed language (e.g. Racket) is achievable using an automatically generated tracing JIT compiler on RPython framework. You can see the details for all of them in my Professional page.

There has been exciting developments this year (2015) that'll completely change my professional (and personal) life. (while the funding is normal for US colleges, it's a big deal around here in Europe/Turkey)

Amazing!

Now I'm getting prepared to move to Bloomington, Indiana US this summer (July 20th, 2015) to live and study there (hopefully) for a min four years. I'm now working on (for two months) finalizing HazirCevap and wrap up all the work I've done on NLP here at Bogazici and say goodbye. Things are going well. I might even have a couple of weeks to study PL to reiterate on what I've studied 2-3 years ago to prepare before the action.

Finally I'll be able to work on PL; type systems, continuations, relational interpreters, monads, Racket, Haskell.. It's a bit sentimental for me as well, because I did what I did within the previous (5) years only to achieve this. I love challenges, and this is by far the biggest and hardest challenge I have ever faced, because let's be real, doing CS PhD in Turkey is nothing like doing a PhD in US.
